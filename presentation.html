The Promise 03 / 15

"Be your own boss.
Set your own hours."

The gig economy marketed itself as a marketplace of autonomous, flexible work. Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and their peers attracted millions with promises of independence from the rigid 9-to-5.

What they didn't advertise: a sophisticated infrastructure of behavioral control, designed by hundreds of social scientists and data engineers, built underneath the app interface.

HRW, "The Gig Trap," 2025 · Dubal, "On Algorithmic Wage Discrimination," SSRN, 2023

The Machinery 05 / 15

This is not
market forces.
This is design.

Uber is engaged in an extraordinary behind-the-scenes experiment in behavioral science to manipulate [drivers] in the service of its corporate growth.

Noam Scheiber · N.Y. Times · "How Uber Uses Psychological Tricks to Push Its Drivers' Buttons" · Apr. 2, 2017

The company employed hundreds of social scientists and data scientists, experimenting with video game mechanics, graphical cues, and noncash rewards — designed to extend driver hours at times and locations less profitable for the driver.

The Mechanisms 06 / 15

The trap has many parts

Surge Pricing
Phantom surge zones appear on the map near drivers, then shift once they relocate — providing free repositioning at the driver's cost.
Quest Bonuses
Tiered bonus thresholds — "earn $20 for 10 trips, $40 for 15" — calibrated to push drivers past their planned hours toward payouts that rarely hold up mathematically.
Forward Dispatch
A new ride loads automatically before the current one ends, eliminating the natural pause where a driver would decide whether to keep going or stop.
Ratings Pressure
A 4.6 average can trigger deactivation. Drivers cannot see individual ratings, identify who rated them poorly, or appeal algorithmic decisions.
Deactivation
Workers are removed from the platform without notice, severance, or meaningful appeal. The algorithm decides. There is no manager to call.
Renewal Pressure
Time-limited offers and "going offline" warnings manufacture urgency — accept the next ride now or lose an opportunity that may not return.
HRW, "The Gig Trap," 2025 · Scheiber, N.Y. Times, 2017
The Legal Framework 07 / 15

Algorithmic
wage discrimination

Two drivers. Same area. Same hour. Same work. Different pay. This isn't an error or market variation — it is the product, as designed.

Workers are paid different hourly wages — calculated with ever-changing formulas using granular data on location, behavior, demand, and supply — for broadly similar work.

Veena Dubal · "On Algorithmic Wage Discrimination" · SSRN, Jan. 2023, p. 5

The company knows exactly what each worker will accept. The worker can only guess why they earned what they earned. The asymmetry of information is total — and deliberate.

Dubal, V. "On Algorithmic Wage Discrimination." SSRN #4337775, Jan. 25, 2023. Columbia Law Review (forthcoming).
The Invisible Floor 08 / 15

~40% of time
on the job
goes unpaid

"Non-passenger platform time" — hours spent waiting between fares — is not compensated. Platforms intentionally keep more drivers on the road than needed, guaranteeing coverage at zero cost.

$9.74 median hourly pay — city-commissioned study, Seattle (Parrott & Reich, 2020)
$23 Uber-sponsored study, same city, same data — excluding vehicle operating costs

Same dataset. The difference: whether to count the cost of owning and operating the vehicle the driver is legally required to provide.

Dubal, SSRN, 2023 (pp. 13–14) · Parrott & Reich, UC Berkeley, July 2020 · Hyman, Cornell, 2020
The Distribution of Harm 09 / 15

The harm
is racialized

Classification as "independent contractors" strips workers of protections built over decades of labor law. Nothing about the work changed — only what the company chose to call it.

  • 01No employer-paid health insurance, unemployment insurance, or workers' compensation
  • 02No guaranteed minimum wage — effective hourly pay can fall below federal poverty thresholds
  • 03No right to organize under traditional labor law
  • 04Deactivation without notice, severance, or meaningful appeal
  • 05Algorithmic wage discrimination disproportionately targets the immigrant and minority workers who make up the majority of this workforce
HRW, "The Gig Trap," 2025 · Dubal, SSRN, 2023 (pp. 9–10)

Regulatory Capture 12 / 15

The platform
wrote the law

$223M spent by Uber, Lyft, DoorDash & Instacart to pass Prop. 22 in California
9+ U.S. states where algorithmic wage discrimination is now legally sanctioned

Proposition 22 legalized not paying drivers for waiting time — the most expensive ballot campaign in California history, funded entirely by the platforms it benefited. Several tactics used in the campaign were widely documented as deceptive.

Dubal, SSRN, 2023 (p. 18) · HRW, "The Gig Trap," 2025
The Broader Threat 13 / 15

This is a preview

Uber drivers' experiences should be understood not as a unique feature of contract work, but as a preview of a new form of wage setting for large employers.

Zephyr Teachout · Algorithmic Personalized Wages · cited in Dubal, SSRN, 2023, p. 11

Algorithmic wage discrimination has already spread into healthcare staffing, restaurant work, and retail logistics. If it is not addressed directly, it will become the default model for low-wage labor across the entire economy.

Dubal, SSRN, 2023 (p. 11) · HRW, "The Gig Trap," 2025
The Path Forward 14 / 15

What would actually change things

  • 01Reclassify workers as employees — restore minimum wage, overtime, unemployment insurance, and the right to organize (HRW, 2025)
  • 02Algorithmic transparency — workers have a right to understand the systems determining their pay, including how wages are calculated (HRW, 2025; Dubal, 2023)
  • 03Ban algorithmic wage discrimination — Dubal proposes a non-waivable legal prohibition targeting the practice directly (Dubal, 2023)
  • 04Universal social protection — decouple healthcare, workers' comp, and unemployment from employment classification (HRW, 2025)
  • 05Federal enforcement — DOL, FTC, and OSHA regulation of platform labor practices, not just classification status (HRW, 2025)
